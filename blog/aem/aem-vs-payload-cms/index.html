AEM vs Payload CMS: An Honest Comparison

At WegWorks, we've had the privilege of implementing both Adobe Experience Manager (AEM) and Payload CMS for clients across different industries and scales. This comparison isn't about declaring a winner‚Äîit's about sharing our hands-on experience to help you make the right choice for your specific needs.

The WegWorks Perspective

We approach every CMS recommendation by first understanding the client's content team size, technical capabilities, budget constraints, and long-term vision. There's no universally "better" platform‚Äîonly the right fit for your situation. We've seen AEM excel in complex enterprise scenarios and Payload shine in agile, developer-driven projects.

Platform Overview

Adobe Experience Manager (AEM)

AEM is Adobe's enterprise-grade digital experience platform that's been around since 2010 (originally Day CQ5). It's part of the Adobe Experience Cloud ecosystem and is designed for large organizations managing complex, multi-site, multi-language digital experiences.

Payload CMS

Payload is a modern, open-source headless CMS built with TypeScript and Node.js. Launched in 2021, it's designed for developers who want full control over their content infrastructure without sacrificing the authoring experience.

Where Each Platform Excels

AEM Strengths

  • Enterprise-scale multi-site management
  • Visual, in-context content editing
  • Deep Adobe ecosystem integration
  • Sophisticated workflow and permissions
  • Built-in personalization capabilities
  • Comprehensive DAM with smart tagging
  • Regulatory compliance and security
  • 24/7 enterprise support

Payload Strengths

  • Complete code ownership and control
  • Modern TypeScript/Node.js stack
  • Rapid development and iteration
  • No licensing costs (open source)
  • Flexible, customizable admin UI
  • First-class API support (REST & GraphQL)
  • Easy local development setup
  • Strong developer experience
Our Implementation Experience

With AEM: We've deployed AEM for Fortune 500 companies managing 50+ regional sites with complex translation workflows. The Multi-Site Manager and Live Copy features are genuinely powerful‚Äînothing else handles this scale of content governance as elegantly. However, the implementation timeline was 8-12 months, requiring a dedicated team of certified AEM architects.

With Payload: For a fast-growing startup, we built a complete content platform in 6 weeks. The developer experience was exceptional‚Äîour team was productive within days. We integrated it with Next.js and deployed on Vercel. The client's marketing team picked up the admin panel quickly, and we've shipped new features weekly without vendor dependencies.

When to Choose Each Platform

Choose AEM When...

You're an enterprise with 100+ content authors, need multi-site/multi-language at scale, require deep Adobe integration, have budget for enterprise licensing, and need comprehensive compliance/governance.

Choose Payload When...

You have a strong development team, want full control over your stack, need rapid iteration, are budget-conscious, or building a headless-first architecture with modern frameworks.

AEM for Marketing Teams

Non-technical marketers who need visual editing, drag-and-drop components, and in-context preview without developer involvement for day-to-day content changes.

Payload for Developer Teams

Teams that value code-first configuration, want to version control their content models, and prefer building custom solutions over adapting to platform constraints.

The Real Trade-offs

Cost Considerations

AEM's licensing starts around $200K/year and can exceed $1M for large deployments. Add implementation costs ($500K-$2M) and ongoing support. Payload is free to self-host‚Äîyour costs are hosting, development, and maintenance. For a mid-size project, we've seen:

  • AEM Total Year-1: $400K-$800K (license + implementation)
  • Payload Total Year-1: $50K-$150K (development + hosting + maintenance)

Team Requirements

AEM requires specialized talent‚Äîcertified AEM developers command premium salaries and are harder to find. You'll likely need 2-4 dedicated AEM engineers plus Adobe support contracts.

Payload can be maintained by any competent TypeScript/Node.js developer. Your existing web development team can likely handle it without specialized training.

Vendor Lock-in

AEM: Significant lock-in. Your content, components, and workflows are deeply tied to the Adobe ecosystem. Migration away is a major project.

Payload: Minimal lock-in. Your content is in a standard database (MongoDB or Postgres), and the code is yours. You could migrate to another system with reasonable effort.

Our Honest Assessment

After implementing both platforms extensively, here's what we tell our clients:

If you're asking "Can we afford AEM?"‚Äîyou probably can't maximize its value. AEM shines when organizations can invest not just in licensing, but in the people and processes to leverage its full capabilities.

If you're asking "Is Payload enterprise-ready?"‚Äîyes, but with caveats. You'll need to build some things that come out-of-the-box with AEM (personalization, advanced workflows, visual editing). The question is whether building those features costs less than AEM licensing.

Neither platform is "better." AEM is a luxury car with all the features; Payload is a performance vehicle you can customize exactly how you want. Both get you to your destination‚Äîthe right choice depends on how you want to travel.

Questions to Ask Yourself

  1. What's your realistic budget? Include implementation, licensing, hiring, and 3-year TCO.
  2. Who will manage content daily? Technical team or marketing/content team?
  3. How complex is your content model? Simple blog or interconnected experiences?
  4. What's your time-to-market pressure? Months or weeks?
  5. Do you need Adobe ecosystem integration? Analytics, Target, Campaign?
  6. What's your scale? One site or dozens with shared content?
